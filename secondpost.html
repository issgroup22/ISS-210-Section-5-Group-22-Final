Who's Affected by Voter Suppression?

Here's how to fight it.

Posted by Chapin Ramseyer on April 28, 2023

Everyone experiences voter suppression. The people that are affected by voter suppression the most are based on their skin color, age whether young or old, and disabilities; for instance, “Across the country, 1 in 16 Black Americans cannot vote due to disenfranchisement laws.” Another reason people don’t vote is because of them not having a government-issued photo ID. This is evident by the following quote, “25% of the voting age Black Americans do not have a government-issued photo ID.”.

Another factor that plays a role in voting is the number and locations of the voting places. The countries with a larger percentage of minority populations have fewer polling sites which results in less voters. Latinix and Black Americans are less likely to vote as a result of not being able to get off of work during the voting times. Not having enough voting locations leads to having to travel to vote. This is seen in South Dakota as “32 %of Native voters cite travel distance as a factor in deciding whether to vote.” Another factor that affects voting is that people with disabilities are likely to encounter at least one impediment to voting.

The way you can stand against voting suppression is finding a way that works for you. There are many different ways to vote which includes “Automatic, online, and same-day voter registration”. These can lead to encouraging more participation and reduce the chances of error. Another thing that helps with voting is being able to vote early because it helps people that are going to travel or have accessibility concerns to vote.
