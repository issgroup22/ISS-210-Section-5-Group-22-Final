Equal representation

by John O'Hara

American citizens should have equal representation in a vote, no matter the cultural identity. Voter suppression is often seen by many as something of the past. The fall of literacy tests of the Jim Crow era has put the stop to voter suppression against minority groups. Or has it really?

Voter suppression started after the civil war. It mainly targeted African Americans who became free during this time period. Voter suppression in the southern states stemmed from racist laws targeting poor African Americans. The Jim Crow act includes poll taxes and literacy tests. The south also used violence to scare minorities away from the poll boxes. Many of these voter suppression strategies remained in place until the 1965 passage of the Voting Rights Act. Finally, in 2013, the Supreme Court removed key protections of the voting rights act.

The voting system needs to be fixed, because it’s not fair that minorities don’t get the same chance to vote. Within our country the minority population adds up to 127 million people compared to 187 white voters. Minorities have the power to swing elections in DC. Even though voting restrictions are not as strict as they once were, voter suppression is still in effect.

The mid 1800s were filled with laws to prohibit African Americans from voting. In the 1890s the Supreme Court ruled against the civil rights movement countless times. This enabled white democrats to reclaim voting laws. White democrats made a deal with the republican president Rutherford B. Hayes that allowed him to win the south. Hayes withdrew the military from the south, thus effectively ending reconstruction. The next three decades the south produced laws that disenfranchised African-American voters. The US government is still trying to recover from countless corruption, racism and other tactics used to control the polls.
